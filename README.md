# LSI-Framework
LINGUISTIC SECURITY – A FRAMEWORK FOR AI GOVERNANCE
Linguistic Security Institute (LSI)
Founder: Sahara Al-Madi
March 2026

Overview
Linguistic security is a framework for understanding and governing how AI systems interact with human language. It asks a single question across domains: What does technology owe the voices it learns from?
This question emerges not from fear of what AI might become, but from an informed curiosity about how it already works and for whom. Through my research, I have documented how models fail on dialectal Arabic in predictable ways: dialectal bias, "Clever Hans" effects where models are right for the wrong reasons, and gold label inconsistencies that require auditing and correction of training data. These are not merely technical problems. They are governance problems. They reflect systems that lack accountability to the communities whose voices they process.
For marginalized language communities, speakers of dialectal Arabic, Indigenous languages, and other under-resourced varieties this is not an abstract concern. It is a lived reality of misrepresentation, extraction, and erasure. Linguistic security offers a lens for AI governance that centers the relationship between language, power, and consent.

The Governance Problem
Current AI governance frameworks focus primarily on technical metrics:
Accuracy – How often is the model correct?
Fairness – Does performance vary across demographic groups?
Robustness – Does the model fail under adversarial conditions?
These metrics are essential but insufficient. They miss a deeper dimension: the relationship between language and the communities who speak it. When an AI model is trained on human language, it is not merely processing data. It is inheriting cultural context, linguistic variation, and the power dynamics embedded in every utterance.
My research with NAMAA at ACL 2026 reveals what this looks like in practice. Analyzing model performance on Arabic polarization detection across 10+ LLMs (DeepSeek, Qwen, Gemma, Llama, Fanar), I documented patterns of:
Dialectal bias – Models consistently failed on dialectal Arabic varieties underrepresented in training data
Hallucination – Models generated confident but incorrect rationales
"Clever Hans" effects – Models produced correct predictions for wrong reasons, masking deeper failures
Gold label inconsistencies – Auditing revealed systematic errors in ground truth data, requiring relabeling
These are not merely technical problems. They are governance problems. They reflect systems that lack accountability to the communities whose voices they process.

The Framework: Three Pillars of Linguistic Security
Pillar 1: Meaning Transparency
Question: How do models interpret meaning, and can we audit that process?
Meaning transparency requires going beyond prediction accuracy to examine how models arrive at their outputs. This includes:
Rationale evaluation – Auditing the reasoning models generate alongside predictions
"Clever Hans" detection – Identifying cases where models are right for the wrong reasons
Error analysis – Understanding not just that models fail, but why they fail
Gold label auditing – Ensuring ground truth data is actually true
For governance, meaning transparency enables:
Deeper auditing of AI systems
Identification of hidden biases
Accountability for model behavior, not just outcomes
Pillar 2: Data Sovereignty
Question: Who controls data about language, land, and lives?
Data sovereignty asserts that communities should have authority over data derived from their language, their land, and their lives. This principle, long advocated by Indigenous data sovereignty movements, applies equally to:
Dialectal language data – Communities should consent to how their language is represented
Cultural context – Models should not extract meaning without understanding
Non-human signals – Animal vocalizations, environmental soundscapes, and Earth observation data raise parallel questions of sovereignty
For governance, data sovereignty requires:
Moving from consent to ongoing accountability
Developing frameworks for community benefit and reciprocity
Ensuring that AI systems give back to the communities they learn from
Pillar 3: Cross-Domain Accountability
Question: Do the same principles apply across human and non-human contexts?
Linguistic security recognizes that questions of meaning, extraction, and sovereignty extend beyond human language. As AI systems increasingly interpret:
Animal vocalizations – Bioacoustics and wildlife monitoring
Environmental soundscapes – Climate and conservation data
Earth observation – Satellite imagery and land use
The same governance challenges emerge: Who defines meaning? Who benefits? How do we ensure listening doesn't become an extraction?
For governance, cross-domain accountability means:
Applying consistent ethical frameworks across contexts
Learning from Indigenous data sovereignty to inform conservation AI
Building bridges between human language AI and non-human signal interpretation

Applications for AI Governance
For AI Auditing
Linguistic security provides criteria for evaluating not just model performance, but model accountability. Auditors can assess:
Does the model demonstrate meaning transparency?
Was data collected with community consent?
Are there mechanisms for ongoing accountability?
Have gold labels been audited for accuracy?
For Policy Development
Policymakers can use linguistic security to:
Draft regulations that address meaning extraction, not just data privacy
Require transparency about model reasoning, not just outcomes
Center marginalized communities in AI governance
Develop standards for cross-domain accountability
For Industry Practice
Companies can adopt linguistic security to:
Build trust with users and communities
Identify linguistic risks before they become harms
Design systems that are worthy of the trust placed in them
Implement robust error analysis and gold label auditing

Conclusion
Linguistic security is not a replacement for existing governance frameworks. It is a lens and a way of asking better questions. It grows not from fear, but from informed curiosity: a commitment to understanding how AI already works, for whom, and at whose expense. And it is built in the open, through collaboration, shared tools, and accessible knowledge because the best solutions emerge when we build them together.
As AI systems increasingly mediate our relationship with language, land, and each other, these questions are no longer optional. They are urgent. The Linguistic Security Institute (LSI) exists to ask them across domains and to build the frameworks, tools, and communities needed to answer them together.
